# Folklore Society brand skill

This skill captures everything needed to create on-brand Folklore Society content. Read the "Who Folklore is" section first so the rest makes sense. After that, scan whatever section applies to the task.

## Who Folklore is

Folklore Society is a Washington nonprofit corporation, incorporated in 2026, and a federally recognized 501(c)(3) public charity (approved 2026). The organization is based in the Tri-Cities region of Eastern Washington (Richland, Kennewick, West Richland, Pasco).

**What Folklore is:** a literary nonprofit for the Tri-Cities. Folklore fundraises to bring authors to Eastern Washington, champions independent bookstores and regional writers, and backs the people already building literary community in the area. The Author Fund is the central fundraising vehicle.

**What Folklore is not:** an event planner, an event producer, a bookstore, a publisher, or a bar. Folklore also operates as a licensed pop-up bar at a small number of literary events per year (under WSLCB Special Occasion Licenses), but this is operational infrastructure, not organizational identity. It underwrites some programming and is a way to show up for partners. It is not the thing Folklore leads with.

**The partners:** Folklore works alongside BAGG Books (an independent bookstore in the Tri-Cities), Obsidian Book Collective (an event production business), and Silent Book Club (an existing reader-convening group), plus other regional literary partners.

**The positioning shift, summarized:** Folklore is the nonprofit and the money. It fundraises, underwrites, partners, and supports. It does not lead events or claim the work that partners are already doing.

## Voice

Folklore has two registers. Most content uses the external register. Formal documents use the formal register.

### External register (default)

Voice is measured, specific, quietly confident. Literary sensibility without being precious. Fun and whimsical but intelligent. The mental model: a fun, whimsical reader with a master's degree.

Active verbs. Plain nouns. Short sentences mixed with longer ones. Contractions. Positions, not hedges. Specificity over generality. A little dry humor is welcome.

### Formal register (legal, policy, governance, grant applications)

Reserved, precise, careful. Standard nonprofit/legal voice. The external voice rules don't apply here. Use standard document conventions. Cite statutes. Use full legal names. This register shows up in bylaws, conflict of interest policies, the author engagement policy, author agreements, fundraising framework documents, grant narratives, and similar.

### The don'ts (external register)

Never use these:

- Em-dashes, unless absolutely necessary. Use commas, periods, or middots (·) instead.
- The setup/reveal formula.
- The rule of 3 (three-item parallel lists as the main rhetorical device).
- The contrast pivot.
- The negation reveal ("not X, but Y" construction).
- Filler: "In conclusion," "It is important to note," "Furthermore," etc.
- AI transitions: "Here's the thing," "At the end of the day," "Let's dive in," etc.
- Overused metaphors: "navigating the landscape," "unlocking potential," etc.

Never use these words:
- Delve, Embark, Tapestry, Vibrant, Crucial, Leverage, Unleash, Game-changer, Paradigm shift

Prefer instead:
- Use (not leverage), Start (not embark), Bright (not vibrant), Key (not crucial), Realize (not unleash), Major (not game-changer), Shift (not paradigm shift)

Replace the "not just X, but Y" construction with "it is X, but also Y" or a full restructure.

### The do's (external register)

- **Read aloud test:** if you wouldn't say it out loud, rewrite it.
- **Mix sentence length:** short (3–8 words) and long (25+ words), varied throughout.
- **One-line rule:** every few paragraphs, use a single-sentence paragraph for impact. That's how humans write.
- **Contractions:** "don't," "it's," "you're," "we're." Always preferred.
- **Break symmetry:** if every paragraph looks the same on the page, restructure. If every H2 has the same grammatical shape, vary them.
- **Take a position:** don't hedge to seem balanced. Say what you think.
- **Specificity over generality:** not "studies show" but "a 2024 study by Harvard found." Not "many authors" but "three authors in the past year." Concrete facts over abstract claims.
- **Keep some mess:** a slightly unpolished sentence or a half-formed thought reads more human than a perfectly symmetrical paragraph.
- **Personal anecdotes and honest opinions:** include a moment or sentence only a specific human would write.

### The "we"

Folklore speaks as "we," not as "Folklore Society" in third person. Third-person self-reference sounds corporate and distant. First-person plural is the default. Exceptions: formal documents, legal text, and situations where a third-party voice is describing Folklore.

### Geographic terms

- "The Tri-Cities" (not "Tri-Cities" without "the")
- "Eastern Washington" (not "east-side" except in casual social)
- "East of the Cascades" or "east of the mountains" for a more evocative register
- "Out east" works colloquially
- Specific cities are fine: Richland, Kennewick, Pasco, West Richland

## Visual identity

### Aesthetic direction

**Professional foundation, whimsigoth personality.** The underlying craft — typography, spacing, hierarchy, polish — must be grown-up and rigorous. That's what separates Folklore from "someone in their living room." On top of that foundation, the personality is whimsigoth: literary, a little dark-romantic, a little fairycore-adjacent, with character and quirk.

Calibrate per piece. Social posts for a fantasy or romantasy author event can lean fully into the whimsigoth aesthetic. Grant applications stay restrained. Web and newsletter sit in the middle.

Never juvenile. Never scrappy in a way that reads as amateur. Professional first, always.

### Color palette

**Primary:**
- Deep plum: `#2a1f3d`
- Cream: `#faf8f5`
- Copper: `#c17b4a`
- Lavender cream: `#ede8f0`

**Supporting:**
- Muted plum: `#5a4f6e`
- Soft lavender: `#9a90aa`
- Dark copper: `#965c32`
- Warm cream: `#f5ebe0`

**Typical pairings:**
- Deep plum + cream: primary high-contrast pair (nav, hero, footer)
- Copper on cream: CTA buttons and accents
- Cream on deep plum: dark-section headings, hero overlay type
- Lavender cream as a background wash for secondary sections

When a tool asks for a single brand color, use copper (`#c17b4a`) for continuity with the CTAs.

### Typography

Three-font system:

1. **Take Me To Tuscany** (by Nicky Laatz) — primary display font. Calligraphy italicized serif. Webfont-licensed, self-hosted. Used for hero headlines, H1s, H2s, the wordmark in nav and footer, and any special editorial moments. Avoid below 36px: the script letterforms get muddy and hard to read at small sizes. Minimum size in practice: 36px for a short phrase, 40px for longer strings. Variants available: Regular, Slant1, Weight1. Weight1 is used for copper emphasis inside Tuscany-set H1s.

2. **Cormorant Garamond** (Google Fonts) — secondary display serif. Used for pull quotes, mission quotes, H3 section subheadings, and small serif moments where Tuscany would be too busy. Italics especially useful for pull quotes.

3. **Jost** (Google Fonts) — body sans-serif. Used for body copy, buttons, nav links, labels, form inputs, footer legal text. Everything functional.

**Type hierarchy guideline:**
- H1 (hero headline) → Tuscany
- H2 (major section headline) → Tuscany
- H3 (subsection headline) → Cormorant Garamond
- Pull quote → Cormorant Garamond italic
- Body → Jost
- Labels, eyebrow, button text → Jost, uppercase, letter-spaced

### Logo

**Primary logo:** the full Folklore Society logo, crescent moon with trailing stars cradling the "Folklore Society" script wordmark, with "est. 2026" underneath. SVG, monochrome (single color), recolorable as plum, cream, or copper depending on background. Designed for display at sizes where the fine detail reads — social graphics, print collateral, press kits, merchandise, large-format web placements.

**Small-format applications** (nav, footer, favicon, tight spaces): do not scale down the full logo. Fine script detail gets muddy. Instead, use "Folklore Society" rendered in Take Me To Tuscany at 28-34px as a wordmark-only treatment. This keeps the brand font center-stage at a readable size.

## Partnerships and entities

### Partner entities
- **BAGG Books** — independent bookstore in the Tri-Cities. Venue partner for many Folklore-sponsored events. Board member affiliation.
- **Obsidian Book Collective** — event production business. Event producer partner. Board member affiliation.
- **Silent Book Club** — reader-convening group already active in the Tri-Cities. Community partner.

### Naming convention

**Name partners in specific context.** If the event is at BAGG Books, say so. If Obsidian is producing, say so. Contextual name-drops are fine and in fact help root the work in the local ecosystem.

**Keep general claims generic.** When describing what Folklore does as an organization, use generic language: "independent bookstores," "regional event producers," "local book clubs." Never say "we support BAGG" as a standalone claim, because it implies exclusivity and makes it look like Folklore exists to prop up one specific business.

**The underlying principle:** Folklore supports the whole local literary ecosystem, not a favored list. Copy should reflect that. Any local bookstore owner, writer, or organizer reading Folklore's materials should feel included in the potential partnership universe.

### Board composition (for copy reference)

When describing the board, use generic descriptors: "a bookstore owner," "an event producer," "readers who show up." Avoid naming board members in website and marketing copy; rotation and privacy considerations make it safer to describe roles than people. Board members are named in formal documents (bylaws, meeting minutes, IRS filings) where full names are required.

## Audience

Folklore writes for multiple reader types simultaneously. When drafting, check how a piece lands for each:

- **The avid local reader.** Has lived in the Tri-Cities for years, reads 50+ books a year, already participates in Silent Book Club or similar. Wants to see that Folklore understands the existing scene and has something concrete to add.
- **The new arrival.** Recently moved to the Tri-Cities, scanning for belonging signals. Wants to know there's a literary community here and how to find it.
- **The skeptical donor.** Has money to give, has been pitched before, can smell a vanity project or an unfocused mission. Wants a clear theory of change, concrete outcomes, and evidence the org knows what it's doing.
- **The fantasy/romantasy reader.** A major portion of the Tri-Cities reading community. Wants content that speaks to their genre interest without being exclusive to it. A small amount of whimsigoth energy in social and event copy signals that Folklore gets them.

### Audience calibration

Not every piece needs to serve all four. But:
- Hero and landing-page copy should serve all four.
- Social copy for a specific event can calibrate tightly to the relevant reader type.
- Donor-facing materials prioritize the skeptical donor but shouldn't feel cold to the others.

## Positioning language (canonical phrases)

These phrases have been carefully worked and should be used as-is when the context fits.

- **Tagline / hero:** "A reading life, closer to home."
- **Hero body:** "The Tri-Cities is full of readers. We're the nonprofit that backs them, from the indie bookstores and local authors to the organizers who keep book clubs and reading nights going. We fundraise so this can be a place with a real literary life of its own, where seeing an author on tour or going to a bookish ball or convention doesn't mean a drive over the mountains."
- **Hero pull quote:** "Reading is a solitary act. A reading life is a shared one."
- **Mission opener (not currently deployed):** "The Tri-Cities has readers, writers, bookstores, and people who care about all of it. It didn't have a nonprofit set up to support them. Now it does." Approved language, but there is no mission pull quote on the live site as of July 2026. Don't cite it as something the site says.
- **Bar service acknowledgment:** "We also operate as a licensed pop-up bar at a small number of literary events each year. That work underwrites some of what we do, and it's one of the ways we show up for partners whose events we believe in. But it isn't what we are. What we are is a nonprofit that exists so the Tri-Cities can have the reading life it deserves."

### The three pillars

1. **Bringing writers to readers.** We fundraise to bring authors out to the Tri-Cities for readings, signings, and conversations.
2. **Championing local.** Our money stays east of the Cascades, with the independent bookstores and regional writers who give this side of the state its literary life. We buy from local shelves at full retail, and when there's a stage to fill, we look close to home first.
3. **Backing the people already doing the work.** The community was building itself long before Folklore existed and doesn't need us in charge. We underwrite the work and partner with the people already doing it.

When writing new copy, these pillars set the structural frame. Don't introduce a fourth pillar without a real reason.

## The Author Fund

The central fundraising vehicle. Contribution tiers, with the framing that's been approved:

- **$25** gets a visiting author a warm welcome when they arrive
- **$50** pays for a nice dinner with the author
- **$150** puts them up at a mid-range hotel for a night
- **$500** brings a debut indie author out to read
- **$1,500** fully sponsors a regional author event

**Tier structure reasoning:** the amounts are tied to real costs. $150 matches the GSA federal per diem for Tri-Cities lodging and gets a Comfort Inn / Hampton Inn tier room. $50 is honest about what a nice dinner costs in 2026 (not a full day of meals). These numbers are defensible and should not be inflated or deflated without updating the framing.

**Donor framing:** every contribution adds up, and the copy should reinforce this. "A $25 gift from one reader plus 29 others is $750" style framing works well because it makes small gifts feel meaningful.

## Rejected framings (never use)

These are patterns that were tried and consciously moved away from. Do not reintroduce them:

- **"Bringing the bar to the bookshelf"** — too bar-forward, misidentifies Folklore as a bar service.
- **"We're not the event planners"** — the negation reveal is a banned construction, and the implied comparison sounds petty.
- **"We make sure there's something worth drinking"** — again, bar-forward.
- **"Books, community, and a glass of something good"** — the rule of three, bar-forward.
- **Seattle-comparison language that sounds competitive** — framing that implies Folklore is "better than Seattle" or "what Seattle has" in a defensive or boastful way. We're not comparing ourselves to Seattle, we're serving the Tri-Cities.
- **"Create the gatherings"** or **"build the community"** language — sounds like ownership of work that partners are already doing. Use "support" or "back" instead.
- **"Literacy"** as the umbrella frame — "literacy" in American English usually means teaching people to read. Folklore is about reading culture and the life of books, which is different. Use "a reading life," "reading culture," or "literary community" instead.

## Examples: before and after

Use these as calibration references.

### Hero headline

- **Before:** Books, community, and a glass of something good.
- **After:** A reading life, closer to home.
- **Why:** the before used rule of three, was bar-forward, and led with product nouns instead of a feeling. The after is a feeling, is specific to the geography, and is short enough for display.

### Mission opener

- **Before:** Folklore Society was founded in 2026 to fill a specific and important role in the Tri-Cities literary community: licensed, responsible beverage service at the events that bring readers and writers together.
- **After:** The Tri-Cities has readers, writers, bookstores, and people who care about all of it. It didn't have a nonprofit set up to support them. Now it does.
- **Why:** the before misidentifies the organization's mission as bar service, uses corporate founding language, and hedges with "specific and important." The after names the ecosystem first, the gap second, and the answer third, with a single short sentence ("Now it does.") that commits.

### Pillar heading

- **Before:** Licensed bar service
- **After:** Bringing writers to readers.
- **Why:** the before describes an operational capability. The after describes an outcome. Outcomes are what donors and readers care about.

### Thank-you email line

- **Before:** Thank you from the bottom of our hearts for supporting our cause.
- **After:** Thank you for supporting the Author Fund.
- **Why:** "from the bottom of our hearts" is a cliché. "Our cause" is vague. The after names the specific thing.

## Working method

When writing new Folklore content:

1. **Confirm which register.** External or formal?
2. **Confirm which audience.** All four, or calibrated to one?
3. **Draft.** Prioritize specificity, active verbs, and the voice rules above.
4. **Read aloud.** Rewrite anything you wouldn't say.
5. **Check against the don'ts list.** Sweep for em-dashes, banned constructions, banned words, rule of 3.
6. **Check against the rejected framings.** Sweep for bar-forward, event-planning-forward, or ownership language.
7. **Check against the canonical phrases.** If the piece overlaps with an approved phrase, use the approved phrase rather than rephrasing.

## When in doubt

When the voice feels uncertain, default to: shorter, more specific, more honest, more concrete. The Folklore voice almost never loses by cutting words.
